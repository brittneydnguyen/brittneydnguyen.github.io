/\/\/\/\/\

When I woke up from the operation, I found myself tucked neatly under a scratchy, white blanket so thin I could feel the draft from the air conditioner on my thighs. I wiggled my toes---just to make sure they still moved—as my eyes glossed over the barren walls enveloping me. The hospital must not have a budget for interior design, I thought. In fact, the only other thing in this tiny room was a half-melted cup of ice chips sitting on a nightstand next to my bed.

I tapped my fingers---again, just to make sure they still moved—and heard my nails clink against a piece of plastic. To my surprise, I found myself clutching a big, red button. Out its end came a comically long wire running along the edge of the room, connecting the button to whatever was outside of the steel hospital room door. Globs of solidified metal dripped along the door’s edges, as if the staff installed the blasting air conditioner just to stop the steel from melting. This was the best provider in my network, I reminded myself. Out of morbid curiosity, I pushed the button.

Almost immediately, the window panel on the door slid open. Metal grated against metal, producing a sharp screeching sound that even a hawk would shirk away from. On the other side of the window was a figure in a hazmat suit. I couldn’t even make out their eyes.

Operation could not be completed. There is a monster inside of you, the figure said in a tone somehow sharper than the sound of the metal panel sliding instantly shut again. Monster? I thought. I slammed the button several more times in hopes of summoning the figure, but my efforts remained futile. It became clear that no one was coming back for me.

I flung open the paperlike covers and winced in pain from the sudden movement. I wriggled out of my hospital gown and looked down at my bare body. Thick, black stitches zigzagging from the top of my sternum to just above my pelvic bone only haphazardly held my left and right sides together.

Monster?

Without thinking, I slipped my thumb under one of the sloppy loops and gave it a tug. The rough string ripped through my skin, and syrupy blood oozed out from the fresh incision. I didn’t even register the pain—--the only thing I noticed was the way my soft flesh gave out so easily under the pressure of my knuckle. I held the end of the now loose piece of string up to the fluorescent lights and twisted it around my fingers. How silly, I thought, as red dripped down my palm and forearm. The doctor must be mistaken about the monster. I’m only a person.

I shuddered at the image of someone I thought I trusted cutting me open and seeing my intestines writhe around in my belly, only to be so horrified by my innards’ resemblance to unlucky worms on a sidewalk after a storm, that they close me up without even checking to see if they put all the blood back in my body.

Pressure against my abdominal wall snapped me out of my trance. A lump emerged from my upper stomach. I felt a thrumming along my ribs. Waves of vibrations cascaded across my torso.

In a frenzy, I tore out a stitch. Then another. Then another. The more I ripped myself apart, the bigger the lump grew.

Suddenly, blood spattered in my face, and the sound of wet meat slapping against the linoleum tile echoed against my eardrums. I wiped the viscous fluid from my eyes to see small, red tracks running from the bed, across the mopped floor, towards the pristine walls.

The monster bounced across the narrow hospital room with ease. Its long, dark hair shined under the bright lights, shrouding its small, wiry frame. Clinging to its body was what looked like a matching pajama set with faint polka dot patterning.

It never once glanced at me. It was too fixated on the wall in front of us: pacing back and forth, looking up and down at the blank space. I squinted my eyes, craned my neck, and twisted my head in all sorts of angles—--so desperate to try and see whatever it saw. But the more time had passed, the more menacing, black fuzz gathered at the corners of my vision, threatening to permanently paint over the textureless white of the hospital.

After what felt like a lifetime of deliberation, it finally stood still. It stared at a point on the wall just off center, slightly above eye level. Abruptly, it jumped and gave the wall a smack. When it hit the ground again, it stood back to admire its work.

The mark it left behind was vibrant and precise. There was no smudging or smearing or mistaking what I saw: this was a handprint. All five fingers were opaquely filled in, and the palm would have been perfect if it weren’t for the dip in the center that makes a hand so fit for cradling. Monster? I thought.

Our shared moment of silence was interrupted by another burst of energy. It crouched and sprung and squatted and leapt, decorating the entire canvas with handprints. Every so often, it would run its hands through its hair or wipe them on its pajamas, trying to get more pigment to complete its masterpiece. With every new print painted, it let out something like a squeal of delight.

I wanted to understand—--no, I needed to understand. My weary eyes swept again across the hospital room, looking for something, anything to get its attention. My gaze landed on the cup next to me. With all the strength I could muster, I picked up the cup.

It finally swiveled its head in my direction at the sound of the ice chips rattling against the styrofoam. Spotting the cup, it bolted across the room. For a second, I was afraid. I had no idea what it wanted, or if it would hurt me, or if I would still find it alluring once I really looked it in the eyes. For a second, I understood the people who opened me up only to carelessly clamp me closed again moments later. When it reached me, it took the vessel and gave the ice water a sniff. It was so small, it had to hold the cup with both hands. It looked at me for a little, and I suddenly grew self-conscious towards what it thought about me. After it seemed to have made up its mind, it took some tiny steps backwards towards the end of my bed, always keeping its eyes locked on mine. It did a little hop to climb on my mattress and shimmied around to get into a comfortable seat. I did what I could to move my legs, just so it would have slightly more space. When it finally got situated, it took a big sip of the ice water and let out a satiated sigh. As the black fuzz clouded over the rest of my vision, I saw a corner of its mouth lift in a tremble, as if this was the first time it was attempting anything of the sort. This, I knew: she was smiling at me.
